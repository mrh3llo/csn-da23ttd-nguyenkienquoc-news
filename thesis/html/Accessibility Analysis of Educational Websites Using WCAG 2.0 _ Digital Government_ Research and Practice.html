        Abstract

        In recent years, the internet has emerged as the primary information hub, encompassing various domains such as education, healthcare, government, and e-commerce. With a vast user base relying on websites for accessing essential information, ensuring web content accessibility has become imperative. Meeting accessibility guidelines is crucial to accommodate users with diverse abilities worldwide. This article examines accessibility variances among education board websites hosting higher-level school leaving examinations. The study evaluates the landing pages of 13 educational websites from both developing and developed countries, employing six Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) tools endorsed by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C). The study has meticulously compiled the outcomes of each assessment tool, delineating the diverse accessibility concerns encountered on each website. Furthermore, alternative recommendations have been proposed to assist web developers in rectifying these issues. The findings underscore a higher prevalence of accessibility issues within the education board websites of developing countries compared to their developed counterparts. Specifically, the analysis revealed prominent text contrast failures among selected web pages from developing nations, while non-text contrast failures were more prevalent in those from developed countries. Notably, the education board websites of India and Japan emerged with the highest number of accessibility issues among the sampled websites. As a way forward, a case study was shown on using LLM for making websites accessible.

        1 Introduction

        With the rise of the internet, traditional offline platforms have transitioned to online spaces, offering convenient access to global information with just a click. According to Siteefy [1], a digital marketing and SEO resource, there are approximately 1.13 billion websites worldwide, with 18% actively utilized by people globally. Nations worldwide maintain multiple websites, facilitating easy and efficient access to various services, mitigating common issues like long queues and time consumption [2]. However, ensuring website accessibility is crucial to facilitate equal access and opportunities for all individuals, regardless of their abilities. Tim Berners-Lee, the pioneer of the World Wide Web, noted, The power of the Web is in its universality. Access by everyone regardless of disability is an essential aspect [3]. Thus, web designers and developers must prioritize accessibility in their designs, ensuring that websites, tools, and technologies are usable by people with disabilities. Enhanced accessibility not only empowers users with varying abilities but also fosters more effective web usage and contribution. [3]. As accessible websites and software proliferate, individuals with diverse abilities can engage with and contribute to the web more seamlessly. However, despite progress, challenges persist. WebAIM's assessment of home page accessibility for the top 1,000,000 websites revealed that a staggering 96.3% exhibited failures in complying with Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.0. This underscores the ongoing need for heightened attention to accessibility standards to ensure inclusivity and usability for all users Top of Form [4]. Likewise, a concerning report indicated that over 90% of websites in the United Kingdom lack accessibility, posing significant obstacles for individuals with diverse abilities, particularly in their online job search endeavors [5]. This underscores the critical importance of prioritizing website accessibility to ensure that information is readily accessible to every user group.
        Education-focused websites serve as vital platforms for disseminating essential information to a diverse audience comprising students, parents, and educators [6]. However, these platforms must address the unique accessibility needs of users, who may experience challenges due to a range of abilities, including color blindness, hearing impairment, and visual impairments [7]. Indeed, the primary concern with educational websites is ensuring the accessibility of the information they offer. Among school-related websites, there are those specific to individual institutions and those providing universal information applicable across multiple schools. Universal information websites often focus on high-stakes examinations, such as high school leaving exams or board exams. These exams are pivotal in students' lives as they often dictate their future career paths. Typically, examinations are administered at the secondary and senior secondary levels in schools and at the college level for higher education (Figure 1). In order to advance to higher education, students must successfully complete their board or final examinations, marking the culmination of their school-level education. Annually, numerous students nationwide enroll in these board examinations, conducted by respective education boards in each country. Education board websites serve as vital platforms, offering crucial information to students, teachers, and parents regarding examination enrollment, registration, and access to essential resources like timetables, curriculum, syllabi, and results. Ensuring the accessibility of these websites is paramount to guarantee equitable access for all individuals, regardless of their abilities. Therefore, comprehensive accessibility evaluations are essential to ensure inclusivity and enable seamless access to educational opportunities and resources for everyone.

        2 Literature Review

        2.1 Legislations

        There are currently more than 2 billion people with a disability in the world, accounting for 37.5% of the world's population. According to the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), the term disability means an individual has a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more of his/her major life activities or an individual is regarded as having such an impairment. The impairment may be cognitive, developmental, intellectual, mental, physical, sensory, or a combination of multiple factors [10]. In the United States, Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 protects individuals from discrimination based on disability. The non-discrimination act applies to employers and organizations to receive financial assistance from federal departments or agencies. It defines the rights of individuals with disabilities to participate in, and have access to, program benefits and services. The civil rights of people with disabilities were protected by the ADA of 1990 followed by the ADA Amendments Act of 2008 [10, 11]. In India, the Guidelines for Indian Government Websites (GIGW) recommends that web applications follow WCAG guidelines in order for web pages to be used by the widest possible audience [12]. In the United Kingdom, there are multiple acts that deal with conformance to accessibility guidelines by the education websites, like the Equality Act of 2010, Public Sector Bodies (Websites and Mobile Applications) (No. 2) Accessibility Regulations of 2018, and so on [13]. Despite the various acts, rules, and regulations, multiple accessibility issues still exist in education websites that restrict many users from accessing them. In the United States, Section 508 of the U.S. Rehabilitation Act requires federal government websites to be made accessible to people with disabilities. This law is based on World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) Priority 1 checkpoints.

        2.2 Web Accessibility

        The World Wide Web, commonly known as the web, is an information system where documents and other web resources are identified by Uniform Resource Locators (URLs), which may be interlinked by hyperlinks, and are accessible over the internet. Web content generally refers to the information in a web page or web application, including natural information such as text, images, and sounds, and code or mark-up that defines structure, presentation, and so on. The web is fundamentally designed to work for people with diverse abilities with any hardware, software, language, and location. The web has become a basic tool of information, education, and social networking. Regarding the web as a tool for education, it provides quick access to vast information including texts, video and audio, and reference sites like government and historical data. By surfing the web, students have an opportunity to interact with others and share and exchange ideas of common interest. Accessibility, usability, and inclusion are three important factors to be considered while developing a website usable by people with disabilities. In the early days, websites were evaluated manually, as tools were not reliable in flagging issues. Manual inspection methods consumed more manpower and time, thereby being expensive compared to automated tools [79]. There is no way to confirm the expertise of an evaluator, resulting in biased evaluations, and experts may fail to perform a detailed meticulous review. In this regard, the Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI) suggests a set of WCAGs to be followed by developers of any website to make it accessible for people with different ranges of abilities [152]. Guidelines in the W3C are categorized into three levels of conformance in order to meet the needs of people of different groups and different situations: Level A (lowest), Level AA (mid-range), and Level AAA (highest), respectively. Level A forms the most basic success criterion to be met by websites and ensures evaluating if websites have utilized meaningful colors, sequences, and audio controls; Level AA checks the website to meet all conditions mentioned in Level A along with checking for proper labels/headings with minimum contrast and audio descriptions; and Level AAA ensures that the website has successfully met all criteria mentioned in Level A and Level AA with extended audio descriptions, alternate media files, and interruptions. These guidelines are organized around four principles, called the POUR factors. These principles lay the foundation necessary for anyone to access and use web content with ease [68]. POUR is the abbreviation of Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, and Robust. If any of the four principles are not met by a website, then the website is considered not accessible for people with different ranges of abilities. There are a plethora of tools by the W3C available for automatic evaluation of web accessibility with respect to WCAGs. The web accessibility evaluation tools are software programs or online services that help determine if web content meets accessibility guidelines [69].

        2.3 Educational Websites

        Jati and Dominic [14] evaluated the accessibility of 90 randomly selected government, education, and business websites of Malaysia using the Tawdish tool. They classified the identified accessibility errors based on three priority levels. Results noted that 93%, 87%, and 87% of education, government, and business websites contained error priority level 1. The rest of the websites contained error priority levels 2 and 3. In another similar study, Ismail and Kuppusamy [15] evaluated 40 websites including education, health, and e-government websites of northeastern regions of India. They provided analysis reports using tools, namely, EvalAccess and WAVE. It was found that there were fewer priority level 1 errors; however, there was a huge number of accessibility violation warnings. They recommended that web accessibility awareness programs on WCAGs should be provided to web designers and developers. Kesswani and Kumar [16] evaluated the accessibility of top universities and educational institutions websites in countries like the United Kingdom, Russia, China, Germany, and India. The selection of countries was a mix of developing and developed nations. The evaluation used TAW, HERA, and Firefox Accessibility Evaluation Toolbar tools. Results were presented in the graphical form highlighting pass and fail criteria according to WCAG 2.0 of each country's website. Results from the evaluation noted that most of the selected websites followed less than 50% of the WCAG guidelines. Kous et al. [17] evaluated the entry web pages of 17 faculty websites at the University of Maribor using the Web Accessibility Checker tool. The authors aimed to examine the WCAG 2.0 compliance level of faculties' websites between 2018 and 2019. The study presented a comparative analysis of the websites between the mentioned years and noted that in 2019, websites had more accessibility issues than in 2018. The authors also recommended suggestions based on the issues found. Gupta and Singh [18] evaluated 27 university websites in Punjab, India, using TAW and WAVE tools. They aimed to examine problems that were occurring multiple times and identify users who were affected by such problems. A large number of perceivable errors were identified for the A level of conformance. Suggestions were also recommended by the authors based on the results. Recently, an approach called variable magnitude was presented by Kuppusamy and Balaji [19]. The authors aimed to compute the accessibility of the homepages of the top 25 Indian institutions websites for persons with disabilities. A variable magnitude approach was proposed to compute the barrier count of a web page. AChecker and WAVE tools were used to give the scores to compute barrier count based on evaluation components. Recommendations were listed based on results and feedback from students with disabilities. Ismail et al. [20] presented accessibility analysis of 59 higher education websites, 19 websites of Polytechnic and 40 websites of the University of Portugal. The work aimed to provide feedback and suggestions to website developers and designers on making web pages universally accessible. Tools like AChecker, WAVE, and aXe were selected for accessibility evaluation of the websites. Results from the analysis noted that major violations were in alternate text for images and buttons, image contrasts, and links without visible text. More recently, Alhadreti [21] used four tools, namely AChecker, WAVE, PageSpeed Insights, and Similarweb, to evaluate the landing pages of 58 higher education websites of the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia from public and private sectors. The paper's objective was to evaluate the current state of accessibility, performance, and engagement of higher education institution websites. Results showed that only four public institution websites and only one private institution website had no errors and were completely accessible to their users. In a recent case study by Alim [22], 44 college websites were investigated and major accessibility issues were identified using TAW and aXe tools. The authors listed the major accessibility barriers reported by websites in terms of several identified problems, warnings, and success criteria violations. The evaluation noted that color contrast, alternative texts, lang attributes, link availability, and marquee elements were the major issues identified among the websites. Based on the results, suggestions were listed to overcome the accessibility issues. Shawar [23] explored the accessibility of homepages of UK research-intensive universities using TAW, WAVE, and Ell Page Checker tools. It was noticed that the most common violations among all the web pages were contrast errors, missing form labels, and empty links and headings. Also, upon comparing the web accessibility results with similar studies in other countries, UK-selected web pages performed well. Abid Ismail [24] evaluated the university websites of the Jordan, UK, and Arabic regions to measure their compliance with accessibility standards for visually impaired people. For the evaluation of websites, WAVE and Cynthia tools were used. Accessibility errors in websites from Jordan and the Arabic region were found to be 13 times and 5 times higher, respectively, compared to those in the United Kingdom. Studies conducted by Shawar [23] and Abid Ismail [24] highlighted these significant differences in accessibility compliance when examining university websites across these regions. Moreover, a recent comprehensive analysis by  Macakolu assesses prospective student web pages across 330 universities spanning Europe, North America, and Oceania [83]. This study underscores the importance of accessibility, usability, and security in university web pages targeted at prospective students, offering valuable insights into compliance levels with WCAG 2.0 guidelines and recommendations for improvement. In line with previous investigations on web accessibility, the Ismailova and Inal [84] study expands on the global discourse by specifically scrutinizing the accessibility landscape of university websites in Kyrgyzstan, Kazakhstan, Azerbaijan, and Turkey. While preceding research has addressed accessibility challenges in various contexts, Ismailova and Inal's analysis serves as a vital addition, shedding light on the disparities in website accessibility across different regions and emphasizing the persistent need for improvements to accommodate all users effectively [84].

        3 Materials and Methods

        3.1 Selection of Websites

        Different countries conduct board examinations in different ways and at different levels. Some countries like India and Canada [26, 27] have a separate body to conduct board exams, while others don't have separate boards for conducting exams as such exams are conducted by the ministry such as China and France [28, 29]. However, the objective that each student must take final examinations at the end of his/her schooling to qualify for higher education is common across all countries. In our study, we considered evaluating the education board websites for the developed and developing groups of countries. Developed countries are countries that have a high quality of life, developed economy, and technological infrastructure [30, 31]. Developing countries have a less developed economy and a comparatively lower quality of life [30, 32]. We have selected the G7 group for developed and the BRICS group for developing countries to evaluate the accessibility of education boards websites.

        3.1.1 G7 Group of Countries.

        The international  G7 is a coalition of seven countries that have the largest and most advanced economies in the worldthe United States, Germany, Japan, the United Kingdom, France, Italy, and Canada, along with the European Union. The G7 members represent over 46% of the gross domestic product globally based on nominal values [33, 34]. We have considered exploring education boards for each of the following seven countries as presented in Table 3:
        Canada has multiple school boards based on region [27]. The Toronto District School Board is the largest school board in Canada [35]. Thus, we have selected to evaluate the accessibility of the Toronto District School Board.
        In France, students aged between 11 to 15 years take the diplme national du brevet (DNB) assessment to receive certification for successful completion of middle school [36, 37]. Students attend the last 3 years of secondary education and take an examination to receive baccalaurat (BAC) certification [36, 37]. The French Ministry of Education issues the respective certification to qualified students. We have evaluated the accessibility of the website of the French Ministry of Education.
        In Germany, the highest German school leaving certificate is called "Abitur" [38, 39]. Certification is received by completing 12 to 13 consecutive years of schooling of the Gymnasium and by successfully passing the final school-leaving exam, Abiturprfung [40]. In some states of Germany, such as Bavaria, Abitur is centralized, and in other states, Abitur is conducted at the school level [41]. According to some reports and news, Saxony (one of the federal states of Germany) has the best-quality education in terms of students performance [42, 43]. Thus, we have chosen the Saxona curricula website for the accessibility evaluation.
        Maturit is an Italian public high school diploma in Italy. To get the Italian public high school diploma, students must pass the Maturit examination. It is a commission examination prepared by the Italian Ministry of Education [44]. Hence, we have evaluated the Maturit website.
        The Tokyo Metropolitan Board of Education is the education board followed in Japan. The board manages individual school systems within the metropolis and directly addresses all the public high schools in Tokyo [45]. Thus, the website of the Tokyo Metropolitan Board of Education is selected for the accessibility evaluation.
        In the United Kingdom, there are six types of education boards [46, 47] followed in different countries. The Assessment and Qualifications Alliance (AQA) is regarded as the largest exam board in the United Kingdom [46, 48]. Therefore, the AQA website is selected for the accessibility evaluation.
        In the United States, some states have their state exit examination for students to get a high school diploma certificate after 12th grade [49, 50]. According to reports [37], California, Texas, and Florida have seen a huge number of student enrollments in public high school. However, California does not conduct state exit examinations now. Thus, we have selected to evaluate accessibility for the State Board of Education of Texas, which conducts the State of Texas Assessments of Academic Readiness (STAAR) assessment for school students (grades 312) [51].

        3.1.2 BRICS Group of Countries.

        BRICS is an acronym for Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa. Goldman Sachs economist Jim O'Neill coined the term BRIC (without South Africa) in 2001, claiming that by 2050 the four BRIC economies would come to dominate the global economy. The National Institute of Educational Studies and Research Ansio Teixeira (INEP) is the governing body responsible for conducting various examinations at different levels including the Enem. We have evaluated the accessibility of CBSE's academic website. It has substantial enrollment of students and is considered as the widely acknowledged board in India [57, 58].
        China has the Ministry of Education of the People's Republic of China, which organizes exams for academic credentials for higher education. Students can find information related to school activities on the respective website [59]. Hence, we have selected the landing page of the Ministry of Education of the People's Republic of China website for the accessibility evaluation.
        South Africa conducts National Senior Certificate (NSC) examinations annually for grade 12 students. The National Department of Basic Education's website contains information related to curriculum, examination timetables, results, certificates, and so on [60]. We have selected the landing page of the Department of Basic Education to evaluate for accessibility.

        3.2 Selection of Web Accessibility Evaluation Tools

        Web accessibility evaluation tools are software programs or online services that help to determine if web content meets accessibility guidelines [8]. The W3C provides a list of web accessibility evaluation tools that can be used to evaluate the accessibility of any website [8]. In our study, we have considered selecting the tools based on the set of features by the Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI) [61]. The following features (Figure 2) were considered to select six accessibility evaluation tools from the W3C website:
        (1)
        WCAG guidelines followed: Each tool is checked for whether it follows at least one version of WCAG standards like WCAG 2.0, WCAG 2.1, and WCAG 2.2.
        (2)
        Language: Evaluation tools support different languages. We have selected tools that mostly support content in the English language.
        (3)
        Type of tool: Evaluation tools can be plugins (extensions) for authoring tools and web browsers, command line tools, desktop or mobile applications, and online services. We have selected tools that are web applications and extensions.
        (4)
        Supported formats: The selected tools check the accessibility of HTML content and other web technologies, such as WAI-ARIA, CSS, SVG, and PDF.
        (5)
        Assisted by: Some web accessibility evaluation tools can be used in more than one way. We have selected tools that can be used as reports and/or step-by-step evaluation results.
        (6)
        Automatically checks: The scope of what the evaluation tool can automatically test varies depending on the tool. The tools check a single page or entire groups of related pages.
        (7)
        License: The tools are available as open source /free to use, commercial, enterprise, and so on. We have selected tools that are free to use.
        (8)
        Accessibility information: We have selected tools that are accessible so that people with disabilities can use them.
        (9)
        Success criteria: There are three levels of conformance defined by the W3C [62], level A, level AA, and level AAA, respectively. Level A is the minimum level to which every website should conform. Level AA is the sufficient level covering requirements of level A and level AA. Level AAA is the highest level of conformance, including all levels, A, AA, and AAA. According to the W3C, many organizations aim to achieve the AA level [63]. Also, S. Kumar et al. [64] noted that level AA is sufficient to be achieved by the websites. Thus, we have considered the tools that check for at least two levels of conformance, i.e., level A and level AA.
        (10)
        POUR factors: Guidelines and success criteria defined by the W3C are organized around four principles called the POUR factors. These principles lay the foundation necessary for anyone to access and use web content with ease. POUR is the abbreviation of Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, and Robust [65]. We have selected tools that are checked to follow at least two of the four factors.

        5 Way Forward: Use of LLMs for Accessibility

        Ideally, a developer should keep in mind WCAG guidelines for accessibility while developing a website. However, it is difficult to keep a track of all these guidelines at once. Developers can leverage various development aids and plug-ins for making their web applications more accessible. For example, the eslint-plugin-jsx-a11y plug-in in React detects accessibility concerns on components of React, helping the developers to stick to best practices. WordPress has several accessibility plug-ins that can improve WordPress-powered websites accessibilities, like WP Accessibility. Most websites are developed using the modern frontend frameworks like Angular and React. These frameworks create a build of the code, which is a compiled version of all the files combined. The compiled version boils down to HTML, CSS, and JS files, which are then rendered by any web browser. Any web accessibility evaluation tool like WAVE works on this compiled version of the website. It is better to encourage use of plug-ins at the time of development, like eslint-plugin-jsx-a11y, which runs for the static code. Researchers earlier also proposed using users interaction simulation [85] and user modelling [86] tools to understand and visualize accessibility problems.
        With the advent of Generative AI, several Large Language Models (LLMs) have come into the picture. They are available as Google Gemini, Claude, ChatGPT, and so forth. For smaller applications, developers can potentially use any of these tools to improve accessibility for the web page. Figures 15 and 16 show examples of using Claude to optimize the web page for accessibility.

For larger codebases, we have tools like AI-driven code assistants such as Google Duet, Github Copilot, and Codeium. These code assistants can be added as plug-ins to Integrated Development Environments (IDEs) like VsCode where we can ask it to make the application accessible. However, these tools have to work with certain limitations for subjective use cases. For example, the content of alttext is highly subjective. Image descriptions for specific images require more practice and understanding of the context. We can skip alttext for decorative images entirely.

        6 Conclusion

        This study focuses on identifying accessibility differences in education-related websites of developing (BRICS) and developed (G7) groups of countries using six accessibility evaluation tools. Based on the results, we noted that the BRICS group of countries reported the maximum number of accessibility issues when compared to the G7 group of countries (Table 7). Among the group of BRICS countries, the CBSE website of India was identified to have the maximum number of issues by the four tools, namely Access Assistant Community, Siteimprove Accessibility Checker, TAW, and Utilitia Validator (Table 7).
        Table 7.
        Similarly, for the G7 group of countries, the Tokyo Metropolitan Board of Education website of Japan was noted to have the maximum number of multiple issues reported by two tools, namely Siteimprove Accessibility Checker and TAW (Table 8). Additionally, the selected websites of the BRICS group of countries reported text contrast failure, while the G7 group of countries websites reported non-text contrast failure. The tools TAW and Utilitia Validator reported some common issues, namely, Navigable: Link purpose (link only) and CSS validation error for both BRICS and G7 groups of countries, respectively.
        Table 8.
        Overall, from this study, we can infer that education board-related websites for both developing and developed countries lack accessibility in some sense. A similar evaluation can be done in the future, and improvements are suggested for other countries websites. It is suggested to use more than one accessibility evaluation tool in order to successfully identify and evaluate the accessibility of any website. Further, web developers can consider the alternative suggestions outlined in our study to improve accessibility of the selected websites. However, it is essential to acknowledge the limitations of this study. First, the selection of evaluation tools might not cover the entire spectrum of accessibility issues, potentially overlooking certain aspects. Additionally, the focus solely on education-related websites may not fully represent the broader accessibility landscape of each country. Future research could address these limitations by employing a more comprehensive set of evaluation tools and expanding the scope to include a wider range of websites beyond those of education boards. Furthermore, investigating the efficacy of specific accessibility improvement strategies and their impact on user experience could contribute to more targeted interventions for enhancing web accessibility globally.
